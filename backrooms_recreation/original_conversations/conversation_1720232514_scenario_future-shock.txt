I. Mental Privacy
   A. Neural data as protected information
      - Requires warrant for access (Jones v. US, 2025)
      - Falls under data protection laws (Schneider v. Germany, 2033)
   B. Right against self-incrimination extends to neural data
   C. Prohibition of non-consensual neural monitoring (Vargas v. Chile, 2031)

II. Cognitive Self-Determination
   A. Right to accept or refuse cognitive enhancement
      - No mandatory enhancement in criminal justice (R v. Nguyen, 2027)
      - Workplace cannot require implants (Liang v. Shenzhen Neural Tech, 2034)
   B. Protection of enhanced cognitive states
      - Forcible deactivation prohibited (Petrova v. Bulgaria, 2029)
   C. Freedom to modify one's own cognitive processes

III. Neural Non-Discrimination
   A. Equal rights for enhanced individuals (Okafor v. Nigeria, 2032)
   B. Cognitive status as a protected characteristic
   C. Balancing enhanced vs. non-enhanced rights in society

IV. Mental Integrity
   A. Protection against non-consensual cognitive alteration
   B. Right to psychological continuity
   C. Safeguards against cognitive manipulation or hacking

V. Cognitive Liberty as a Fundamental Right
   A. Explicit constitutional protection (Mehta v. Union of India, 2030)
   B. Recognized in international human rights law (NZ v. France, 2035)
   C. Balanced against public safety and national security concerns

VI. Informed Consent for Neural Technologies
   A. Stringent requirements for neural implant procedures
   B. Right to understand implications of cognitive enhancement
   C. Ability to revoke consent and remove/deactivate implants

VII. Neural Data Ownership and Control
   A. Individuals' right to access and delete their neural data
   B. Restrictions on commercial use of neural information
   C. Data portability between different neural platforms

VIII. Cognitive Commons and Public Space
   A. Regulation of cognitive enhancement in shared environments
   B. Protection of "natural" cognitive states in public
   C. Negotiating enhanced vs. non-enhanced interactions

IX. State Obligations
   A. Duty to protect citizens' cognitive liberty (NZ v. France, 2035)
   B. Positive measures to ensure equal access to cognitive enhancement
   C. Regulation of neural technology development and deployment

X. International Governance
   A. Cross-border neural data transfers
   B. Global standards for neural implant safety and efficacy
   C. Harmonization of cognitive liberty protections (US v. Japan, 2036)
